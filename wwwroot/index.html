What am I looking at?

This chart shows when players leave reviews. The x-axis is playtime on a log scale, the y-axis is review count.
Blue bars are positive reviews. Orange bars are negative.
Gray bars are reviews edited more than a week after posting (players came back to change their verdict, marking them as fence-sitters whose original opinion shifted). They're still binned by current sentiment, but treat them as uncertainty margins.

Healthy games have negative reviews clustered early. People who don't like it figure that out fast. Positive reviews spread across longer playtimes.

Extractive games show the opposite. Negative reviews come after positive ones. Players feel burned after investing significant time.

The dashed lines show median playtime for positive and negative reviews.
Half of positive reviews came before the blue line, half after. Same for orange. In a healthy game, the negative median sits LEFT of the positive median: the game is being honest about the audience it's for. When negative sits far to the RIGHT of positive, that's extraction. People played for hundreds of hours before admitting they weren't having fun. The "game" wasted their time successfully before letting them realize it.

The 2-hour refund window

Steam lets you refund a game if you've played less than 2 hours. Sounds consumer-friendly until you realize what it incentivizes.

Developers know about the 2-hour window. So they front-load the good stuff. The first 2 hours are polished, exciting, full of promise. The extraction mechanics, the grind, the paywalls come later. After you're locked in.

Look at any extraction game's curve. The negative reviews don't cluster before the refund threshold. They cluster at 10, 50, 500 hours. Long after you've lost the right to get your money back.

The refund window isn't consumer protection. It's a design constraint that teaches developers exactly how long they need to keep up the illusion.

Why doesn't Steam show this?

Three reasons.

Publisher leverage. Show data that makes EA look bad, EA threatens to leave. Steam can't afford that.

Self-incrimination. Valve's own games are some of the worst offenders. Dota 2, CS2, TF2. They're not going to build a feature that puts themselves on the wall of shame. They want you to think that "Very Positive" means that it's a good game (whatever that means). And they want you to stop thinking right there.

The market loop. Steam credits are denominated in dollars but can never become dollars again. Every market transaction takes 5% at best, over 15% at worst. Buy an item, sell an item, buy another... each cycle bleeds value to Valve until it all evaporates and players put new dollars in. Games that keep you playing longer generate more transactions. The extraction isn't just your time. It's infinite taxation on a closed loop. That's how one buys a yacht.

How do I use this?

Share this tool with your friends.

Game companies optimize for metrics that matter. Right now they optimize for engagement, retention, time-to-first-purchase, ARPU. These are the metrics investors and executives look at.

If enough players start checking games here before buying, the shape of the curve becomes a metric that matters. Games with healthy curves sell better. Games with extraction patterns get called out.

You don't need to boycott anything. You don't need to write angry tweets. Just make the data visible. Natural selection does the rest.
